## The failure mode: adverse selection, cleanly quantified

This is *not* the "fill% too low" branch and not the "hit-rate-bound" branch from the failure guide — fill rate is healthy at ~62%. It's the toxic-fill branch, and the `missed`/`hit_m` diagnostic nails it:

- **up6 @ 0.1%:** the 26.5% trigger-time hit rate splits into **13.2% among filled** vs **47.8% among unfilled** (whose gross endpoint averaged +13.8 bps — the winners you never got).
- **dn6:** 13.7% filled vs 49.0% missed.
- Even seed-unanimity — the 49.7%-hit subset that made the breakeven arithmetic look reachable — collapses to **27.2% filled vs 80.0% missed** (up6 @ 0.01%).

The economic logic closes: run.007's case for maker entry was "full-maker 4 bp RT drops breakeven hit to ≈49%, and unanimity achieves 49.7%." But posting a limit doesn't sample the trigger distribution — it samples the *pullback-conditional* distribution, where the hit rate is ~13–27%, nowhere near breakeven. The signal fires on momentum onset; a pullback deep enough to fill you is itself evidence the move died.

Both pre-registered rescue routes also failed:

- **wait×δ grid:** every cell negative (sim −13 to −18); deeper δ only lowers fill% without improving filled-hit — the adverse selection gets worse, not better, the more price improvement you demand.
- **h2-timed hybrid** (the explicit run.009 candidate check in the failure guide): up6 −10.37 with 84.5% routed taker (it degenerates to plain taker), dn6 −15.11. Not positive → not a candidate.

## The second promise: entry-delay stress — a genuinely useful null

taker+1bar barely moves anything: up6 −10.04 → −10.36, dn6 −12.93 → −12.76 (CIs overlap almost completely). So run.007's taker numbers were **not** an artifact of the zero-latency entry assumption. The edge is latency-robust — it's simply ~10–13 bps below water on fees, honestly.

Fold 2's regime collapse repeated (filled hit 2.4%/6.2% vs 9–20% elsewhere), same pattern as run.007. Non-overlapping 1-BTC maker trades: −$47k/−$40k, worse than the taker equivalents.

Three execution levers have now each been tested with pre-registered gates and each returned a clean answer:

1. **Exits** (run.007): TP/SL-at-θ ≈ hold-to-endpoint at short horizons — worth ~nothing.

3. **Latency** (run.008): not the problem.

The per-trade loss is therefore not an execution problem — it's a **prediction problem**: 26.5% trigger hit vs ~58.5% taker breakeven. # Appendix — Q&A and reference tables (discussion of 2026-07-10)

## Why the gate is h6 @ 0.1% (and why run.008 didn't change it)

Two layers. First, **why pre-register one cell at all**: the notebook prints hundreds of numbers (12 heads × 3 rates × variants + grids); with that many cells some look good by luck. One exact configuration was declared "the result" before running; everything else is exploratory — allowed to generate hypotheses, not evidence.

Second, **why h6 = 90 s specifically** (chosen in run.007, per the config comment): the sweet spot between three constraints —

1. *Long enough that θ=20 bp is reachable*: at h2/h3 (30–45 s) the base rate is ≲0.3%, too few positives to train; at h6 it's ~2.5–2.8%.
2. *Short enough that the move doesn't revert*: ~90% of θ-touches survive to the h6 endpoint vs ~10% at run.006's h40 (the finding that killed run.006).
3. *Long enough that entry timing isn't everything*: at 30–45 s a one-bar delay eats the move (h2/h3 rows are marked upper bounds); at 90 s the maker sim can wait 2 bars for a fill and keep ≥4 of 6 bars for the move.

Run.008 kept it byte-frozen deliberately — one variable per run (taker → maker execution only), so any change is attributable to the fill model. Switching the gate to h8 or unanimity after seeing run.007's tables would be exactly the cherry-picking pre-registration exists to prevent.

## KPI glossary (plain words)

- **θ** — the target move size (e.g. 20 bp = 0.20%); "success" = price touches entry ± θ within the horizon.
- **Base rate** — how often the θ-move happens with no model (dumb-luck floor). Grows with horizon.
- **Trigger rate @0.1%** — the model fires only on the top 0.1% most confident bars ≈ 1 in 1,000 ≈ ~10 trades/day.
- **Hit%** — of the fired moments, how often the move actually came (h6: 26.5% ≈ right 1 in 4).
- **Lift** — hit% ÷ base rate, the skill multiplier (9.4× = the model's moments contain the move 9.4× more often than random). Proves ranking skill; says nothing about profit.
- **AP (pooled)** — ranking quality over the *whole* score range: sort all bars by confidence, measure how the true moves cluster at the top (1.0 = perfect, = base rate if random). Broader cousin of lift.
- **IC** — rank correlation between the regression head's predicted return and the actual return. **Daily t-stat** — is the IC reliably positive day after day (|t|>2 = real, not luck).
- **Sim bps** — the money number: average P&L per trade in bps of notional from the full TP/SL/timeout simulation, fees included. Must be > 0.
- **Endpoint bps** — simpler variant: enter at trigger, close at horizon, taker fees. Sim ≈ endpoint here ⇒ exits add nothing.
- **[95% CI, day-boot]** — uncertainty band around sim from resampling whole days (trades within a day aren't independent). Gate demands the entire band > 0.
- **Fill%** — share of signals whose resting limit actually traded (62%).
- **hit_f / hit_m** — hit rate of filled vs missed signals. hit_m ≫ hit_f = the limit catches failures and misses winners = adverse selection.
- **EV/trig** — total P&L spread over ALL signals (unfilled = 0); the fair maker-vs-taker comparison since an unfilled order costs nothing.
- **missed** — average gross move of the unfilled signals (the winners that got away, +13.8 bp).

## How a simulated trade ends (what's inside sim bps)

Concrete walkthrough: an up-trade at h6 (90 s horizon, θ = 20 bp), BTC at $100,000, so 20 bp = $200.

**The setup at entry.** The model fires an "up" signal at some 15-second bar. The sim enters (taker at the close, or via the maker limit in run.008's cell 10M) and immediately brackets the position with three possible endings:

```
$100,200  ← TAKE-PROFIT: resting limit sell (maker)
$100,000  ← entry
$ 99,800  ← STOP-LOSS level
              ...and a clock: 6 bars (90 s) max
```

From then on the sim watches each 15 s bar's **close** and ends the trade at the first of:

**Exit 1 — Take-profit (the "hit", ~26.5% of trades).** If some bar closes at or above $100,200, the resting limit sell is assumed filled **at exactly $100,200** (not the close). A resting limit is a maker order, so fees are cheap: 7 bp round trip. Profit: **+20 − 7 = +13 bp** (+$130 per $100k). Capped — even if price rockets to +50 bp, you got out at +20.

**Exit 2 — Stop-loss (~40% of the non-hits).** If a bar closes at or below $99,800 first, you exit as a **taker** — at that bar's **actual close**, not at the stop level. If price gapped down and the bar closed at $99,700, you eat the whole gap: −30 bp, plus 10 bp taker fees. That's why the average stopped trade costs ≈ **−30 bp**, noticeably worse than the nominal −20.

**Exit 3 — Timeout (~60% of the non-hits).** If 6 bars pass without a close through either level, force-close as taker at `close[entry+6]`. Price wandered somewhere between −20 and +20 bp, on average roughly flat, so the trade costs ≈ the **10 bp taker fees plus drift**.

**Why the average is −10 bp:**

```
26.5% hits     × (+13 bp)   =  +3.4
73.5% non-hits × (−18 avg)  = −13.5   (mix of −30 stops and −10 timeouts)
                              ─────
                              ≈ −10 bp per trade
```

That one line is the economic verdict: the win is too small and too rare to pay for the losses plus the fee toll. Breakeven needs ~58% hits; the model delivers 26.5%.

**Three fine-print conventions:**

1. *Closes only.* The sim never looks inside a bar. A spike to +25 bp and back within one bar without closing above TP = no fill (conservative). Flip side: a within-bar dip to the stop followed by a rally to TP is invisible and scores as TP. The two biases point in opposite directions.
2. *Ties go to the stop.* A single violent bar closing through both levels counts as SL, not TP.
3. *The clock starts at the signal, not the fill.* In the maker version, 2 bars spent waiting for the limit leaves only 4 bars before timeout — hesitation isn't free; the sim charges for it.

**What this machinery proved: exits are almost irrelevant.** Remove the stop entirely: −10.6 vs −10.0. Tighten it to 0.5·θ: same ballpark. Raise TP to 1.5·θ: 0.5 bp better. Hold to horizon with no brackets (the endpoint column): −9.4. Every configuration lands within ~2 bp of every other, because there's only ~1–2 bp of gross edge on the table and fees take 7–10 — no way of slicing the exit changes that. That's the run.007 lesson that pushed run.008 toward entry price, and run.008's lesson (adverse selection) then closed that door too.

## Ideas assessed and why they don't rescue v1 features

**Lower θ (15 bp instead of 20)?** Worse, not better. The fee is a fixed toll (~7–10 bp) regardless of move size, so smaller targets raise the *required* hit rate: a 15 bp hit nets only +8 while a stop still costs ~−22. Already tested implicitly — h2/h3 use θ=15 bp and lose the same −10 to −12. The squeeze is two-sided: fees from below, rarity from above (bigger moves at 90 s are untrainably rare) — that's exactly why θ=20 was chosen.

**Signal-following exit ("hold while the sliding prediction stays positive")?** Three measured facts against: (1) the prediction has no information past ~2 min (IC +0.038 @30 s → +0.003 @2 min, t=1.6) — holding "while positive" beyond the horizon is holding noise, positive half the time by chance; (2) outside the extreme tail the score is weak (IC 0.02–0.04) and flips sign constantly → either churn (run.005: churn fees swamp everything) or add hysteresis and it converges to the existing time-stop; (3) the ceiling argument below. Closest cousin already tested: run.005's score-exit overlay — gate failed, exits locked in local bottoms.

**Trailing stop instead of fixed TP?** Needs winners that run; these mean-revert (~90% of touches survive 90 s but only ~10% survive 10 min). Indirectly tested: TP at 1.5·θ improved up6 by 0.5 bp, worsened dn6. Trailing exits are taker (10 bp RT vs 7 maker), so a winner must run past θ + trail + 3 bp just to match the fixed TP. Practical note: Binance's native trailing-stop minimum callback is 0.1% = 10 bp = half the target move; a meaningful ~5 bp trail is indistinguishable from 15 s bar noise. Becomes interesting only if schema-3/4 catches *cascades* that trend for minutes.

**The exit-rule ceiling (applies to all of the above):** even an omniscient exit that sells every trade at its best moment gives ≈ 0.265·(+13) + 0.735·(−5, non-hit MFE minus taker fees) ≈ **0 bp**. The theoretical optimum over all exit rules is roughly breakeven — exits redistribute path capture, they cannot add edge. This is why every exit variant lands within 2 bp of every other. The problem is prediction quality at entry (26.5% hit vs ~58% breakeven), not trade management.

**Multiple LSTMs at different horizons?** Already built in: one trunk with heads at six horizons + 3-seed ensembles per fold. Separate networks per horizon = more capacity, and capacity is cleared as the bottleneck twice over (run.002's 7.4M-param model < dummy; XGBoost ≈ same 0 IC — two families agreeing means features are the ceiling). Cross-model consensus also tested in close form: seed-unanimity lifts hit 26.5% → 49.7% and still loses (needs ~58%, and the maker route around that died of adverse selection here); the h2→h6 hybrid was negative. Consensus removes noise, it doesn't add information — all models read the same 39 v1 features. Cheap future add: a cross-horizon agreement filter as one exploratory cell in the schema-3/4 rerun.

**Leverage?** Multiplies the sign, never changes it: −10 bp per trade on notional × 10× leverage = −1% of equity per trade, ~10 trades/day. The project already simulates USDT-M futures (fees, prices) — leverage is just position sizing on the same market. **Fees are charged on notional, not margin**, so they leverage up in exact proportion to P&L; every ratio that matters (breakeven hit, bps per trade) is leverage-invariant. What leverage adds at 15 s scale: liquidation risk (at 50–100× a sub-1% gap wipes margin before the −30 bp stop executes — and the signal fires on exactly the cascade moments that gap) and worse stop fills. Funding ≈ neutral at 90 s holds. Leverage is the prize collected *after* a positive per-trade edge exists (then 2–5× turns a small edge into real return on capital); until sim bps > 0, optimal leverage is zero.
